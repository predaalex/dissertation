\section{Dataset Description: FER2013 Enhanced}

The facial emotion recognition model was trained using FER2013 Enhanced dataset, available on Hugging Face as \textit{abhilash88/fer2013-enhanced} \cite{fer2013_enhanced_2025}. It is based on the original FER2013 dataset, which was introduced in the Challenges in Representation Learning competition \cite{goodfellow2015challenges}. I decided to use this enhanced version because it contains extra information that were relevant during training.

FER2013 Enhanced contains 35,887 grayscale face images. Each image has a size of 48x48 pixels and is labeled with one of seven emotion classes: angry, disgust, fear, happy, sad, surprise, and neutral. It is split into training, validation, and test subsets, with the number of images in each split presented in Table~\ref{tab:fer2013-enhanced-splits}.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrr}
        \hline
        Split & Number of images & Percentage \\
        \hline
        Train & 25,117 & 70.0\% \\
        Validation & 5,380 & 15.0\% \\
        Test & 5,390 & 15.0\% \\
        \hline
        Total & 35,887 & 100.0\% \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{FER2013 Enhanced dataset split sizes.}
    \label{tab:fer2013-enhanced-splits}
\end{table}

The dataset is not balanced across classes, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:fer2013-enhanced-overview}. The happy class has the largest number of samples, while disgust has far fewer samples than the other classes. This matters because a model trained directly on an imbalanced dataset can learn to prefer the larger classes. For this reason, class imbalance is considered during the experimental process.

FER2013 Enhanced also provides a quality score for each image. This score is computed from four image properties: edge content, contrast, focus or sharpness, and brightness balance. Edge content and contrast each contribute 30\% of the score, focus contributes 25\%, and brightness balance contributes 15\%. The quality score distribution is also shown in Figure~\ref{fig:fer2013-enhanced-overview}.

The quality score is useful because it allows experiments that compare training on all samples with training only on samples above a minimum quality threshold. The experiments use two filtering thresholds: 0.35 and 0.5. The threshold 0.35 is a permissive filter that removes the weakest images while keeping most of the dataset. The threshold 0.5 is stricter and tests whether training on cleaner images can improve the model. These thresholds are not treated as fixed rules for the dataset, they are used to measure the trade-off between data quantity and data quality. Table~\ref{tab:quality-threshold-retention} shows the number of samples that remain after applying each threshold.

\section{Data Loading, Preprocessing, and Augmentation}

The dataset is loaded in the training script using the Hugging Face \textit{datasets} library. The default dataset name is \textit{abhilash88/fer2013-enhanced}.

Before an image is passed to the model, it is converted to RGB. The dataset images are grayscale, while the pretrained MobileNetV2 backbone expects three input channels. The label is read from the \textit{emotion} field and used as the target class. The training script can also limit the samples used in an experiment. A dataset fraction can be selected for shorter runs, and a minimum quality score can be applied to remove low-quality samples. These options make it possible to compare different training settings without modifying the dataset files.

Different image transformations are used for training and evaluation, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:image-transforms}. The training split uses random crop, horizontal flip, and rotation. The validation and test splits use deterministic transformations, so reported metrics are not affected by random input changes.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{llll}
        \hline
        Step & Training & Validation \& Test \\
        \hline
        Resize & 232x232 & 232x232 \\
        Crop & Random 224x224 & Center 224x224 \\
        Horizontal flip & Yes & No \\
        Rotation & Up to 10 degrees & No \\
        Tensor conversion & Yes & Yes \\
        Normalization & ImageNet stats & ImageNet stats \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Image transformations used during training and evaluation.}
    \label{tab:image-transforms}
\end{table}

The random transformations are applied only during training. Validation and test images are processed with the same fixed resize, crop, tensor conversion, and normalization steps in every run.

\section{Model Architecture}

The classifier uses MobileNetV2 as the backbone. It was designed as a lightweight convolutional neural network for mobile devices and resource-limited systems. IT is built around inverted residual connections and linear bottleneck layers, while also using depthwise separable convolutions to reduce computation. \cite{sandler2018mobilenetv2}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{MobileNetV2_architecture_overview.png}
    \caption{MobileNetV2 architecture overview.}
    \label{fig:mobilenetv2-architecture}
\end{figure}

I selected MobileNetV2 because the project is intended to run locally and was trained on a single GPU. The model is used for live webcam classification, so inference speed is important. The assistant has other local components, such as speech-to-text and LLM inference, so the emotion classification model should not be unnecessarily large. TorchVision reports MobileNetV2 with 3.5M parameters and 0.30 GFLOPS for ImageNet inference \cite{torchvision_models}. Table~\ref{tab:backbone-comparison} shows several possible backbones with similar or commonly used sizes.

Some alternatives have better ImageNet accuracy, but they also increase parameter count or computation. For my project, MobileNetV2 gives a suitable balance between model size, inference speed, and transfer learning performance.

\section{Training Pipeline}

The training pipeline was implemented in PyTorch and all experiments were run on a local machine with an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070 Ti GPU with 12 GB of VRAM. This hardware was sufficient for training MobileNetV2 and matched the local execution direction of the application.

Training starts from the ImageNet-pretrained MobileNetV2 weights. The original classifier is replaced with a dropout layer followed by a linear layer with seven outputs, one for each emotion class. The rest of the network is reused as a visual feature extractor.

The pipeline supports two training strategies. In the baseline strategy, the MobileNetV2 feature extractor is frozen and only the new classification head is trained. In the fine-tuning strategy, the model is trained in two stages. First, only the classification head is trained. Then, the last blocks are unfrozen and training continues with a smaller learning rate.

The optimizer used in both strategies is AdamW. The loss function is cross-entropy, with optional class weights when class-weighted loss is enabled. These weights are computed from the class distribution of the training split. The scheduler observes validation macro-F1 and reduces the learning rate when the score stops improving.

After each epoch, the model is evaluated on the validation split. The training loop records loss and accuracy, while validation also computes macro-F1 and per-class precision, recall, and F1-score. The best checkpoint is saved whenever validation macro-F1 improves. Early stopping is used when validation macro-F1 does not improve for multiple consecutive epochs. Automatic mixed precision is enabled to reduce memory usage. Gradient clipping is also applied before the optimizer step to keep training stable.

\section{Experimental Strategy, Evaluation, and Model Selection}

To determine the best performing model, training was split into multiple sections. The first goal was to choose a good strategy for class imbalance. The second goal was to check whether filtering low-quality samples improves the model. After these two decisions, I used the selected settings in hyperparameter sweeps for baseline training and fine-tuning.

The main metric used for model selection was validation macro-F1. Accuracy was also recorded, but macro-F1 was more important because the dataset is imbalanced. A model can obtain a good accuracy by performing well on frequent classes, while macro-F1 offers equal importance to each class.

\subsection{Comparing Imbalance Tactics}

The first comparison used the same training parameters and changed only the imbalance strategy. The experiment was run on a dataset fraction of 0.2, so the comparison could be completed faster. The three tested strategies were no imbalance handling, class-weighted loss, and weighted sampling.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrr}
        \hline
        Imbalance strategy & Validation macro-F1 & Validation accuracy \\
        \hline
        None & 16.82 & 29.27 \\
        Weighted sampler & 23.16 & 30.94 \\
        Class-weighted loss & \textbf{24.07} & 26.20 \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Comparison of imbalance handling strategies.}
    \label{tab:imbalance-strategy-comparison}
\end{table}

The best strategy from this comparison was used in the following experiments. This kept the later sweeps focused on model and training hyperparameters instead of repeating the same imbalance decision.

\subsection{Comparing Quality Filtering}

The second comparison used the same training parameters and changed only the minimum quality score. The tested values were no quality filtering, 0.35, and 0.5. This comparison was also run with a dataset fraction of 0.2.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
        \hline
        Minimum quality score & Remaining data & Validation macro-F1 & Validation accuracy \\
        \hline
        0.50 & 20.9\% & 20.04 & 25.65 \\
        0.35 & 86.3\% & 24.96 & 31.32 \\
        No threshold & 100.0\% & \textbf{27.02} & \textbf{31.97} \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Comparison of quality score filtering thresholds.}
    \label{tab:quality-filtering-comparison}
\end{table}

The purpose of this step was to measure whether removing lower-quality samples helps more than it hurts. A stricter threshold can make the data cleaner, but it also removes many training examples.

This made the experiment faster and gave a reference point for how much performance could be obtained from the pretrained features without changing the convolutional backbone. In the fine-tuning sweep, the model was trained in two stages. The classifier head was trained first, then later MobileNetV2 blocks were unfrozen and training continued. This sweep used the same general search setup as the baseline sweep, but also included fine-tuning-specific choices such as the fine-tune learning rate, fine-tune weight decay, and the block from which the backbone was unfrozen.

\subsection{Selected Model}

The selected model is the checkpoint with the best validation macro-F1 after comparing the baseline and fine-tuning sweeps. The test split was used only after the model was selected. On this split, the model obtained a test macro-F1 of 66.05\% and a test accuracy of 67.42\%.

Table~\ref{tab:fer2013-prior-methods-comparison} places this result next to FER-2013 results reported by Naik et al. \cite{naik2026efficientnetb2}. The selected MobileNetV2 model is slightly above the reported VGG16 result, while using far fewer parameters. It does not reach the accuracy of the EfficientNetB2 model from the cited paper, but it keeps the model smaller, which is important for local real-time use.
